This retelling presents the Trojan War through Patroclus‚Äôs experience, reshaping the conflict as a story of personal sacrifice rather than military triumph. Events traditionally celebrated for their heroism are reframed to highlight their emotional cost.

By shifting perspective, the retelling challenges traditional ideas of glory and suggests that love and loyalty are as powerful as strength on the battlefield.

Madeline Miller uses retelling to shift the perspective of the Trojan War narrative to Patroclus' point of view. In the original Illiad, Achilles is described as proud, ego-driven, violent, and fierce--defined largely by his honor. Rarely being depicted as soft or shy. In The Song of Achilles, however, Miller reinterprets Achilles' character, presenting him as loving, kinder, more morally reflective and emotionally open. Because the novel is written as if Patroclus himself is the one retelling the story, the portrayal of Achilles feels more deeply personal and intimate almost as if it was seen through rose colored glasses, formed by Patroclus' love and affection towards Achilles.
